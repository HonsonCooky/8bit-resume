WHAT IS A SOFTWARE ENGINEER?

An Observation By: Harrison Cook

10/09/2024

Note: There exists a section at the end of this article that explores edge cases and other ideas that detract from the focus of what I'm trying to communicate.

Observations

As communities grow, the divergence in understanding among their members inevitably increases. This phenomenon can be observed throughout history, from ancient civilizations to modern societies. The larger a group becomes, the more interpretations and perspectives it encompasses, leading to a gradual shift from the original meaning. This can be starkly seen in the evolution of religions, where foundational principles and teachings have splintered into countless sects and interpretations.

Similarly, in today's rapidly expanding tech industry, the role of a "Software Engineer" has lost a concrete understanding. Initially, this role demanded a deep understanding of electronics due to technological limitations. However, as a result of technological advancements across the board, the tech industry has grown and evolved, where the role now encompasses a wide range of specializations and skills.

With this community being no different than others, this inevitably results in conflicting ideas and understandings. I believe the differences among community members can be understood when acknowledging the differences in shared qualia - commonality in personal / subjective interpretations of code (and its relationship to hardware). In essence, programming can be viewed through various lenses: "Computer Scientists," "Software Engineers," and "Software Developers," each with distinct yet overlapping understandings.

The Spectrum

Expanding upon this idea, the creation of abstraction tools (frameworks, libraries, programming languages, etc) has removed the requirement for an understanding beyond the code. Without a requirement to understand the underlying mathematical and electronic components, there emerges another form of programmer - which I believe has been improperly labeled as a "Software Engineer".

A quick history of computing through the lens of this context will help outline this spectrum. Initially, programming was mathematical theory (Alan Turing). Technological advancements then enabled theory to be put into practice (IBM machines, Fortran), and eventually abstracted away from the programmer (C#, Ruby, JavaScript). We could place markers along a spectrum of programmers as such:

  • Theoretical - Computer Scientists

    Based heavily in mathematics, Computer Scientists thrive on the ideas of code more-so than in the implementation of it. Where Lambda Calculus and Featherweight Java are modeling languages - tools that enable the expansion of computing through theory.

  • Practical - Software Engineers

    At some point, we inevitably attempt to apply theory into practice, which is where Software Engineers find their calling. With an intimate understanding of hardware, delving into environments where no tools exist to help you is where the detail-oriented minds of Software Engineers can thrive.

  • Abstract - Software Developers

    Bringing forth the possibilities of electronic hardware to the rest of the world requires a breadth of knowledge across a wide array of mediums. For Software Developers, thriving on the abstract enables the implementation and management that drives the utility of computers to non-technical users.

A Continuum

To be crystal clear, the markers above exist as transitions along a continuum.
